Abstract / Introduction

During the Winter 2026 semester, I worked as a Student IT Technician with Computing and Communications Services, specifically as part of the Managed Desktops team at the University of Guelph. In this role, I supported networked Windows and macOS computers, assisted clients with hardware and software issues, worked with enterprise tools such as Active Directory, Intune, and MECM, and gained hands-on experience in both academic and clinical IT environments through my work with the Ontario Veterinary College. This report reflects on my work term experience, the skills I developed, the goals I worked toward, and the lessons I will carry into future technical and professional roles.

Information About the Employer

The University of Guelph is a major academic institution that supports teaching, research, administration, and campus life through a wide range of services. Computing and Communications Services, commonly known as CCS, is one of the largest IT departments on campus and provides central information technology support to many of the university’s academic and service departments. CCS supports essential technology services such as internet connectivity, Gryph Mail, calendaring, Office 365 applications, central login accounts, software distribution, and technical support for students, staff, and faculty.

Within CCS, the Managed Desktops team focuses on supporting the personal computing infrastructure used by departments across the university. This includes managing Windows and macOS devices, supporting software and hardware installations, troubleshooting technical issues, maintaining device inventory, and helping clients use university technology effectively. During this work term, I worked with the main Managed Desktops team in Johnston Hall while also spending much of my time supporting the Ontario Veterinary College. This gave me the opportunity to work in both educational and clinical settings, where technology must support teaching, research, administration, and specialized equipment or software needs.

Job Description

As a Student IT Technician with CCS Managed Desktops, my role involved supporting a broad range of technologies used by University of Guelph departments. Much of my work focused on diagnosing and resolving issues with networked Windows and macOS computers in an enterprise environment. This included software installation, hardware troubleshooting, printer and peripheral support, client training, inventory updates, ticket documentation, and assisting with device rebuilds or replacements. I also gained experience using tools and processes that allow large organizations to manage many computers in a secure, consistent, and policy-driven way.

A unique part of this work term was my time supporting the Ontario Veterinary College. While the core responsibilities were similar to other Managed Desktop work, the OVC environment introduced additional complexity because I was supporting both faculty and clinical spaces. Some devices were used for teaching and administrative work, while others supported more specialized software, equipment, or workflows. This helped me understand how IT support must adapt depending on the needs of the department, the type of user, and the role the technology plays in daily operations.

This position required a combination of technical knowledge, communication, teamwork, and adaptability. Some skills, such as general troubleshooting, operating system knowledge, and logical problem solving, connected to what I had learned through school and previous work experience. However, many of the most important skills were developed on the job, especially working with enterprise tools, understanding university-specific systems, communicating with clients, and documenting issues clearly in a professional environment.

Goals / Learning Outcomes / Reflection

One of my main goals for this work term was to develop a deeper and broader understanding of the systems and technologies used in a university IT environment. I wanted to understand not only how tools such as Active Directory, Intune, and MECM are used, but also why they are configured in certain ways to support security, software deployment, device management, and user access. Through hands-on troubleshooting, asking questions, reviewing workflows, and observing experienced analysts and technicians, I gained a much stronger understanding of how these systems work together in a large organization.

Another goal was to improve my problem-solving ability by developing a more structured approach to technical issues. Coming from a previous Help Centre role, where more complex issues were often handed off, this position gave me the opportunity to follow problems from start to finish. I learned to break issues into smaller parts, gather the right information, ask clarifying questions, identify possible root causes, and apply solutions more independently. Over time, I became more confident troubleshooting unfamiliar problems and adapting my approach depending on the client, device, or environment.

My third major goal was to improve my technological literacy and communication skills. I wanted to become better at explaining technical issues clearly and professionally while using appropriate terminology. Working directly with clients helped me learn how to adjust my language depending on their level of technical understanding. I became more comfortable explaining what was happening, why certain steps were needed, and what the client could expect next. This helped clients better understand their issues and sometimes allowed them to provide useful details they may not have originally considered relevant.

4 Month Reflection

This work term was a valuable opportunity for me to grow both technically and professionally. Working as a Student IT Technician with CCS Managed Desktops allowed me to build on my previous IT experience while gaining a deeper understanding of enterprise tools, university systems, and the support processes used to manage a large number of devices across campus. My time supporting the Ontario Veterinary College also gave me experience in a more specialized environment, where technology supported teaching, administration, research, and clinical work.

Throughout the term, I became more confident in troubleshooting issues independently, communicating with clients, and adapting my approach based on the situation. I learned that strong IT support is not only about technical knowledge, but also about patience, clear communication, teamwork, and understanding the needs of the people using the technology. Overall, this term helped me become a more capable and professional technician, and it gave me skills and experience that I can carry into future work terms and my career.

Conclusion

Overall, this work term helped me grow both technically and professionally. I developed a stronger understanding of enterprise IT systems, improved my ability to solve problems independently, and became more confident communicating with clients in a clear and professional way. Working in both the main Managed Desktops environment and the Ontario Veterinary College gave me a broader view of how IT support operates across different departments, especially when technology needs to support academic, administrative, and clinical work.

If someone were to describe the contents of this report, I would want them to say that it shows meaningful growth through real hands-on experience. This work term was not only about fixing computers or resolving tickets; it was about learning how large IT systems are managed, how to support clients effectively, and how to approach problems with patience, structure, and professionalism. The experience has prepared me well for future work terms and has strengthened the skills I will continue to build throughout my career in computing and technology.
